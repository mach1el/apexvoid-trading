## Step 1 — Establish Higher-Timeframe Bias

Before marking any OB on your execution timeframe, determine the dominant direction on the higher timeframe (HTF). If you trade the 15-minute chart, your HTF is the 1-hour or 4-hour.

- **Bullish HTF bias** — Look only for bullish OBs (long entries) on the lower timeframe.
- **Bearish HTF bias** — Look only for bearish OBs (short entries) on the lower timeframe.

This single filter eliminates the majority of low-probability setups. Trading against the HTF trend using OBs is a consistency killer.

## Step 2 — Identify the Swing Structure

On your execution timeframe, map out the recent swing highs and swing lows. You need a clear structure to work with:

- In an uptrend, price makes **higher highs (HH)** and **higher lows (HL)**.
- In a downtrend, price makes **lower lows (LL)** and **lower highs (LH)**.

Mark the most recent strong swing high and strong swing low. These define the current dealing range and help you determine whether price is in a **premium** (above equilibrium) or **discount** (below equilibrium) zone.

## Step 3 — Locate the Displacement Move

Scan for an **impulsive, displacing move** that breaks through a significant swing point. This is the move that confirms institutional intent.

Signs of valid displacement:

- Two or more consecutive full-bodied candles in one direction
- Little to no overlap between candle bodies
- A clear **break of structure (BOS)** — price closes beyond the previous swing high (bullish) or swing low (bearish)
- The move creates a visible **fair value gap (FVG)** or imbalance on the chart

If the move is grinding, overlapping, or lacks conviction, skip it. Weak moves do not produce reliable OBs.

## Step 4 — Mark the Last Opposing Candle

Trace back from the start of the displacement to find the **last candle that closed in the opposite direction**:

- **For a bullish OB**: Find the last **bearish (down-close) candle** before the bullish displacement began.
- **For a bearish OB**: Find the last **bullish (up-close) candle** before the bearish displacement began.

This candle is your order block. It represents the final moment where counter-trend participants were active before being overwhelmed by institutional flow.

### What if there are multiple opposing candles?

If there is a cluster of opposing candles (e.g., three consecutive bearish candles before a bullish move), you have two options:

1. **Conservative** — Use only the last single candle closest to the displacement. This gives a tighter zone and better risk-to-reward.
2. **Aggressive** — Use the entire cluster as the OB zone. This gives more room for price to react but widens your stop.

Choose based on your risk tolerance and backtest results. Neither approach is universally superior.

## Step 5 — Define the Zone

Draw a rectangle on your chart to mark the OB zone:

- **Bullish OB zone**: From the **open** of the bearish candle to the **low** of the bearish candle. Some traders extend to the wick low for added buffer.
- **Bearish OB zone**: From the **open** of the bullish candle to the **high** of the bullish candle.

The zone represents the price range where institutional orders were filled. Your entry, stop-loss, and position sizing all reference this zone.

## Step 6 — Validate With Context

Not every correctly drawn OB is worth trading. Before committing capital, run through a validation checklist:

- **Does the OB sit in a premium or discount zone that aligns with your trade direction?** Bullish OBs are stronger in discount; bearish OBs are stronger in premium.
- **Did a liquidity sweep precede the OB formation?** OBs formed after a sweep of equal highs/lows, session levels, or trend-line liquidity are higher probability.
- **Is the OB on or near a higher-timeframe point of interest?** Confluence with an HTF OB, FVG, or key level adds conviction.
- **Has the OB already been mitigated?** If price has already returned to and traded through the zone, it may be consumed. First-touch OBs are preferred.

<Callout type="trap" title="Forcing OBs Where None Exist">
If you find yourself stretching definitions to justify an OB — using tiny candles, ignoring weak displacement, or marking zones in the middle of a range — step back. Not every move has a tradeable order block at its origin. Forcing setups leads to overtrading and inconsistency. If the chart does not present a clean OB, wait.
</Callout>

## Timeframe Considerations

The process is identical across timeframes, but the character of OBs changes:

| Timeframe | OB Characteristics |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Monthly / Weekly** | Massive zones. Used for long-term directional bias, not precision entries. |
| **Daily / 4H** | Strong structural significance. Ideal for swing trade entries or defining HTF POIs. |
| **1H / 15M** | Common execution-timeframe OBs. Suitable for intraday setups with tight risk. |
| **5M / 1M** | Very refined entries within HTF zones. Requires fast execution and discipline. Use for sniper entries only. |

Always mark OBs on your HTF first, then drill down to the LTF to refine entry within those HTF zones.

<KeyTakeaways>
- Start by establishing HTF bias — only look for OBs that align with the dominant trend direction.
- Displacement and a break of structure are the minimum requirements to validate any OB.
- The OB candle is the last opposing-close candle before the impulsive move — trace back from the displacement start.
- Define the zone from the candle's open to its extreme (low for bullish, high for bearish).
- Always validate with context: premium/discount location, liquidity sweeps, HTF confluence, and mitigation status.
- If the setup is not clean and obvious, do not force it. Wait for the next opportunity.
</KeyTakeaways>
