# PD Arrays Overview

**PD Arrays** (Premium/Discount Arrays) are ICT's framework for classifying price levels and zones where institutional order flow is expected to enter the market. "PD" refers to **premium** (above equilibrium — expensive) and **discount** (below equilibrium — cheap). The arrays themselves are specific structural elements on the chart that act as magnets or reaction points for price.

---

## The Core Idea

Price moves between extremes. Between any swing high and swing low, there is a midpoint — the **equilibrium** (50% level). Everything above equilibrium is considered **premium**; everything below is **discount**.

- **Smart money sells in premium** — distributing positions at elevated prices.
- **Smart money buys in discount** — accumulating positions at depressed prices.

PD arrays are the specific chart features within premium or discount zones where this activity is most likely to occur.

---

## The PD Array Matrix

ICT organizes PD arrays into a ranked hierarchy. When price is retracing, it is expected to react at the first unmitigated PD array it encounters — but if that array fails to hold, price continues to the next one in the hierarchy.

<CompareTable title="PD Array Hierarchy (Bullish Retracement — Discount Arrays)">

| Rank | PD Array | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Order Block (OB)** | Last down-close candle before a displacement up. Highest-priority reaction zone. |
| 2 | **Fair Value Gap (FVG)** | Three-candle imbalance. Price often returns to fill or partially fill before continuing. |
| 3 | **Breaker Block** | A failed order block that, once broken, acts as support/resistance from the opposite direction. |
| 4 | **Mitigation Block** | A previous failed setup that gets revisited and "mitigated" as part of a new move. |
| 5 | **Rejection Block** | Wicks into a zone without body closes — indicates rejection at that level. |
| 6 | **Liquidity Void** | An area where price moved so fast there are no candles with overlapping ranges — price tends to return to fill. |

</CompareTable>

For **bearish retracements** (premium arrays), the same concepts apply in mirror: the last up-close candle before displacement down becomes the premium OB, and so on.

## Premium vs. Discount in Practice

<ConceptCard title="Where to Buy, Where to Sell" icon="⚖️">
  <p>The PD array framework is fundamentally a <strong>value framework</strong>: buy cheap, sell expensive.</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Bullish bias:</strong> Look for long entries at <strong>discount</strong> PD arrays (OBs, FVGs below equilibrium).</li>
    <li><strong>Bearish bias:</strong> Look for short entries at <strong>premium</strong> PD arrays (OBs, FVGs above equilibrium).</li>
    <li><strong>Equilibrium (50%):</strong> Not an entry zone — it's the dividing line. Entering at equilibrium means you have no statistical edge from the PD framework.</li>
  </ul>
</ConceptCard>

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="trap" title="Common Trap">
  <p>Trading <strong>every</strong> PD array without regard to context. A discount OB inside a bearish HTF trend is not a high-probability long — it's a counter-trend trap. PD arrays must be read within the context of the current market structure and bias.</p>
</Callout>

    <li><strong>Premium</strong> = above equilibrium (sell zone). <strong>Discount</strong> = below equilibrium (buy zone).</li>
    <li>The hierarchy matters: price reacts at the <strong>first unmitigated array</strong> it reaches, or continues to the next.</li>
    <li><strong>Order blocks</strong> and <strong>FVGs</strong> are the most commonly referenced PD arrays.</li>
