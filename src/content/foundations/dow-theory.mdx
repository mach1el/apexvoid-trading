# Dow Theory: The Foundation

<ConceptCard title="Dow Theory" icon="🏛️">
Formulated by Charles H. Dow in the late 19th century, Dow Theory is the foundational framework upon which all modern technical analysis—including Price Action, SMC, and ICT—is built. It posits that market behavior is not random, but moves in identifiable, fractal trends.
</ConceptCard>

Before you learn about Order Blocks, Fair Value Gaps, or complex liquidity engineering, you must understand the rules that govern market movement at its most basic level. The 6 core tenets of Dow Theory are as relevant today as they were over a century ago.

---

## 1. The Market Discounts Everything

The first tenet states that **current price reflects all available information**. Economic data, earnings reports, interest rate decisions, and even mass psychology are already "priced in" to the chart.

When a major news event occurs (like CPI or NFP), the market's *reaction* to the news is more important than the news itself. If a heavily bullish news report causes the market to sell off aggressively, it tells you that institutions used the liquidity of retail buyers to distribute their long positions. The chart always reveals the truth.

---

## 2. The Market Has Three Trends

Dow identified that markets move in a fractal nature, consisting of trends within trends.

<StepFlow>
  <Step title="1. Primary Trend (The Tide)">
    The overarching direction of the market, lasting from several months to years. This is the macro bull or bear market. Trading against the Primary Trend is historically the fastest way to lose capital.
  </Step>
  <Step title="2. Secondary Trend (The Waves)">
    Counter-trend corrections within the Primary Trend, typically lasting weeks to months. If the Primary Trend is bullish, the Secondary Trend is the bearish pullback that provides the next entry opportunity.
  </Step>
  <Step title="3. Minor Trend (The Ripples)">
    Short-term fluctuations lasting days to a few weeks. These are often viewed as "noise" by macro investors, but they form the structural setups (BOS/CHoCH) that day traders and swing traders capitalize on.
  </Step>
</StepFlow>

---

## 3. Primary Trends Have Three Phases

Every major market cycle undergoes three distinct psychological and mechanical phases. Note how closely this mirrors the modern SMC concept of the **Accumulation / Manipulation / Distribution (AMD)** cycle.

- **Phase 1: Accumulation.** "Smart Money" (institutional investors) quietly build positions against the prevailing public sentiment. In a bear market, this occurs at the absolute bottom when retail traders are terrified and selling at a loss.
- **Phase 2: Public Participation.** The trend becomes obvious. Price begins moving aggressively (Displacement) as trend-followers and technical systems join the move. This is the longest and most profitable phase.
- **Phase 3: Distribution (Excess).** The general public—driven by greed and hype—finally enters the market. Smart Money uses this influx of retail buying liquidity to quietly sell (distribute) their positions. Shortly after, the trend collapses.

---

## 4. Averages Must Confirm Each Other

In Charles Dow's time, this meant the Industrial Average and the Transportation Average had to move together to confirm a healthy economy (if goods are being manufactured, they must be transported). 

In modern trading, this tenet is applied through **correlation analysis**:
- If EUR/USD is making new highs, GBP/USD should also be making new highs, and the DXY (US Dollar Index) should be making new lows. 
- If one asset fails to confirm the move of its correlated counterpart (often called SMT Divergence in ICT terminology), it suggests the move is a false breakout or a liquidity grab.

---

## 5. Volume Must Confirm the Trend

Volume is the fuel of the market. It represents the conviction behind a price movement.

If price is breaking to new highs but volume is steadily declining, the trend is running out of institutional participation and is vulnerable to a reversal.

---

## 6. A Trend Persists Until a Confirmed Reversal

A trend is assumed to be in effect until it gives a *definite* signal that it has reversed. 

<Callout type="trap" title="The Reversal Trap">
The most common mistake retail traders make is trying to predict the absolute top or bottom of a trend. They short strong uptrends because price feels "too high." **Do not fight the Primary Trend.** Wait for structural failure—a clear Change of Character (CHoCH) and a lower low—before assuming the trend is dead.
</Callout>

<KeyTakeaways>
- The chart prices in all fundamental news; trade what you see, not what you think should happen.
- Always identify the Primary (HTF) trend before taking a trade on the Minor (LTF) trend.
- Institutional Accumulation and Distribution phases drive major market cycles.
- Correlated assets and volume provide necessary confirmation to price action.
- Assume the trend is your friend until it definitively breaks structure.
</KeyTakeaways>
