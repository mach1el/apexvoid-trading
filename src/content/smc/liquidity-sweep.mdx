# Liquidity Sweep / Stop Hunt

<ConceptCard title="Liquidity Sweep" icon="🎯">
  A **liquidity sweep** (also called a stop hunt or liquidity grab) occurs when price trades beyond a key swing high or swing low — triggering the clustered stop-loss orders resting there — and then **quickly reverses** back through the level. The sweep is the mechanism by which institutional participants obtain the counterparty fills they need to build or unload positions.

  On a candlestick chart, a sweep often appears as a **long wick** (shadow) that pierces a prior high or low while the candle body closes back inside the prior range. The wick represents the moment liquidity was taken; the close back inside confirms rejection.
</ConceptCard>

## Anatomy of a Sweep

<AnnotatedChart title="Liquidity Sweep in Action" showSweep={true} />

The chart above highlights a classic sweep pattern. Price pushes past a prior swing level, triggering resting stops, then reverses sharply. The key elements to observe:

1. **The target level** — a well-defined swing high or low with visible stop-loss clustering.
2. **The pierce** — price trades through the level, often on a single aggressive candle.
3. **The rejection** — price closes back below (for a BSL sweep) or above (for an SSL sweep) the level, forming a prominent wick.
4. **The displacement** — the reversal move is typically impulsive, showing institutional commitment to the new direction.

## Why Sweeps Happen

Institutional order flow operates under constraints that retail traders do not face. A fund manager looking to buy 2,000 contracts of ES futures cannot place a single market order without moving the price against themselves. Instead, they need **resting sell orders** to buy against.

The largest concentration of sell orders sits just below obvious swing lows — in the form of long-position stop-losses and breakout sell-stops. By allowing (or engineering) a move into those stops, institutions effectively convert retail panic into their own entry liquidity.

This is not conspiracy — it is a natural consequence of order book mechanics and the need for counterparty volume at scale.

## Spotting a Sweep in Real-Time

Identifying a sweep as it happens requires attention to three factors:

- **Context:** Is price approaching a level where stops are likely to rest? Look for obvious swing highs/lows, equal highs/lows, or trendline touches.
- **Aggression of the move into the level:** A sharp, impulsive drive into the stops (especially during high-impact news) often signals an engineered grab rather than genuine breakout momentum.
- **Post-sweep behaviour:** The most critical confirmation. A candle that sweeps a high and closes *below* it — especially with displacement — is a strong signal that the move was a grab, not a breakout.

<Callout type="trap" title="Common Mistake: Fading Every Wick">
  Not every wick beyond a swing level is an institutional sweep. In trending markets, price legitimately breaks and holds beyond prior structure. The difference lies in **what happens after the pierce**. A sweep is only confirmed when price reverses with conviction — ideally producing a CHoCH (Change of Character) on a lower timeframe. Blindly fading every wick leads to counter-trend losses in strong moves.
</Callout>

<KeyTakeaways>
  - A liquidity sweep is a **wick beyond a key level** that triggers stops before reversing.
  - Institutions use sweeps to obtain counterparty fills for large positions.
  - Confirmation requires price to **close back through** the swept level with displacement.
  - Always combine sweep identification with structural confirmation (BOS or CHoCH) on a lower timeframe.
  - Sweeps are highest-probability when they occur at higher-timeframe points of interest, not random intraday swings.
</KeyTakeaways>
