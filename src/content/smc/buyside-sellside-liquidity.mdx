# Buy-side & Sell-side Liquidity

<ConceptCard title="Buy-side & Sell-side Liquidity" icon="💧">
  Liquidity, in the SMC framework, refers to **clusters of resting orders** — primarily stop-losses — that sit above swing highs (buy-side) and below swing lows (sell-side). These pools act as magnets for institutional order flow because large players need the opposite side of the trade to fill their positions.

  - **Buy-side liquidity (BSL):** Stop-losses from short sellers placed above swing highs, plus breakout buy-stop orders from traders anticipating a continuation higher. When price trades above a swing high into BSL, those stops trigger as market buy orders, providing sell-side fills for institutions looking to distribute.
  - **Sell-side liquidity (SSL):** Stop-losses from long traders placed below swing lows, plus breakout sell-stop orders. A sweep below a swing low into SSL triggers these stops as market sell orders, giving institutions the buy-side fills they need to accumulate.
</ConceptCard>

## Why Liquidity Matters

Markets are not driven by retail breakout entries. Institutional participants require **counterparty liquidity** to execute large orders without excessive slippage. A bank looking to sell 500 lots of EUR/USD cannot simply hit the bid — it needs willing buyers. The densest clusters of willing buyers sit just above obvious swing highs in the form of buy-stops.

This creates a repeating cycle:
1. Price builds structure (swing highs and lows form).
2. Retail traders place stops beyond those swings.
3. Smart money drives price into those stops to fill their own orders.
4. Price reverses once the liquidity is absorbed.

## Identifying High-Probability Liquidity Pools

Not all swing highs and lows hold equal liquidity. The most attractive targets are:

- **Equal highs / equal lows:** When price forms two or more highs (or lows) at nearly the same price, the cluster of stops is denser.
- **Obvious higher-timeframe levels:** Weekly or daily swing points accumulate stops from multiple participant classes.
- **Extended consolidation ranges:** The longer price respects a level, the more stops build beyond it.

<Callout type="trap" title="Common Mistake: Trading the Breakout">
  Many traders buy the break above a swing high expecting continuation. In reality, the move above the high often **is** the liquidity grab — not the start of a trend. Wait for a sweep *and* a structural shift (CHoCH or BOS) before committing to a direction. A wick above a high that closes back below is far more informative than the break itself.
</Callout>

<KeyTakeaways>
  - **Buy-side liquidity** sits above swing highs; **sell-side liquidity** sits below swing lows.
  - Institutions target these pools to fill large orders against the resting stops of retail traders.
  - Equal highs/lows and higher-timeframe swing points hold the densest liquidity.
  - A sweep of liquidity followed by a structural shift is a higher-probability signal than a breakout alone.
  - Always ask: *"Where are the stops?"* before determining directional bias.
</KeyTakeaways>
